# Open Knowledge — redraft outline (critical-literacy version)

Working spec for a new draft of the deck. Branch: `redraft-critical-literacy`.
Nothing here is built into `index.html` yet.

## Audience
Open Knowledge Fellowship — doctoral students who *teach* at CUNY, in an OER /
open-pedagogy program. **New to AI**, and broadly uneasy about it on values
grounds. Not fluent; not hostile so much as wary.

## Format
~30-minute talk, **then a separate ~30-minute discussion.** The talk is pure
presentation; the closing slide launches the discussion. Design the talk to
*leave threads open* for that conversation rather than resolving everything —
the talk gives the precision tools, the discussion applies them to their own
courses. Three presenters (Stefano, Zach, Steve), one segment each.

## Purpose
Give novices enough understanding to **judge appropriate AI use in OER work,
including the choice to refuse**. Teach AI in general, critically. Not a lab
showcase — the lab is the messenger and a credential, not the subject.

## Thesis
Critical AI literacy = **precision, not a predetermined verdict.** The enemy is
sloppy thinking in *both* directions — uncritical hype and uncritical refusal.
We're neither evangelists nor alarmists. We get precise about each worry — what
holds up, and where the popular hype-and-panic framing overstates it — *with* the
room, not at it.

## Design principles
- Meet the discomfort with honesty, not performance. We don't share most of the
  audience's specific frustrations, so we won't pretend to.
- Concede what's real; correct what's inflated. Both, out loud.
- Assume zero fluency — every term gets a plain handhold or gets cut.
- Show seams and failures, not a highlight reel.
- Leave threads open for the 30-minute discussion.
- Lean on the GC AI Guidelines as the institutional backbone: human agency and
  accountability, room for refusal, disclose use, detectors aren't proof,
  minimal/purposeful use.

## The core: which worries hold up?
For each concern the room carries, separate what holds up from where the *common
public framing* overstates it. The target of correction is the ambient
hype-and-panic discourse — **never the audience.** Voice: "you've heard X; here's
what's actually true," allied with the room, not "your worry is silly." **All
nuanced** — present both sides, don't sloganize.

> Tone guardrail: "overwrought" is working shorthand in this doc only. It never
> appears on a slide or in a presenter's mouth. The deck never characterizes the
> audience's concerns as overwrought, naive, or silly.

| Concern | What's real (concede) | Where the common framing overstates it |
|---|---|---|
| Labor | Power & political economy — deskilling pressure, who profits, data-labor, AI as an austerity/management lever in academia | "It replaces teachers/writers wholesale" — task-shift and pressure, not literal replacement |
| Bias | Reflects and amplifies training-data skews, unevenly across groups | "It's biased, so don't use it" — biased in *knowable* ways; scrutiny beats abstention |
| Corporate concentration | A few firms, dependence, lock-in, opaque governance | The answer isn't abstention, it's public/open alternatives — the thing the lab builds |
| Slop / reliability | Hallucination, fluent-wrong, degrading the info commons | "It's all slop" is as lazy as "it's magic" — the skill is knowing when plausible is enough |
| Academic integrity | Genuinely disrupts assessment and writing-as-thinking | Detector-policing and bans — unreliable and unserious; the work is redesigning assessment |
| Environmental | Hyperlocal water stress from siting + evaporative cooling, opacity, infrastructure cost — a siting/governance issue | Individual-use guilt *and* "aggregate water is an apocalypse" (small vs. power/agriculture) |
| "Theft" / consent | The unease is understandable | Licensing points toward permission; output is yours with real edits; the field's move is reciprocity, not blockade |

Environmental sourcing: Heatmap, "AI data centers' water usage" — real but
hyperlocal; aggregate is small vs. thermoelectric power (~132B gal/day) and
irrigation (~118B gal/day); genuine issue is siting, secrecy, ~$58B in
water-infrastructure cost. https://heatmap.news/plus/the-fight/spotlight/ai-data-centers-water-usage

## Slide-by-slide (~28; ~30 min talk; brisk pace, near the original density)

**Title**
1. AI & Open Educational Resources — fellowship, presenters.

**Part I — What AI actually is (Stefano, ~7 min)**
2. [divider] Part I — What AI actually is.
3. Why we're here, and not selling — goal is to help you judge, including to
   refuse; neither evangelists nor alarmists. (GC guidelines.)
4. You've already used these — ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot; start where they are.
5. What it actually is — it predicts the next piece of text; that's the engine.
6. What it's *not* — not a database, not a mind, not "looking up the answer."
7. Plausible ≠ true — it optimizes for what *sounds* right; hence hallucination,
   and patterns that carry bias. The idea that makes everything else judgeable.

**Part II — What it can actually do now (Steve, ~8 min)** — agentic, kept;
framed as "what it does, and why that raises the stakes," not a showcase.
8. [divider] Part II — From chatbot to agent.
9. Fluent but ungrounded — ask it today's weather: a confident guess with
   nothing behind it.
10. Groundedness — to do better it reaches outside itself: search the web, read
    a file, run code. (the honest answer to hallucination)
11. A tool is a function it can call — "tool use," plainly.
12. "Agentic" means it loops — look, decide, act, repeat; several steps toward a
    goal. The harness runs the loop.
13. Vibe coding — build by describing in plain language; genuinely empowering,
    and exactly why you have to read and check what comes back. (stakes go up
    with capability — bridges to the worries and to OER judgment)

**Part III — Which worries hold up? (Zach, ~9 min, the core)**
14. [divider] Part III — Which worries hold up?
15. The move — let's get precise together: what holds up, and where the popular
    framing overstates it. The sloppy public conversation is the target, not you.
16. Reliability / slop — real vs. "it's all slop."
17. Bias — real vs. "biased, so don't use it" (biased in *knowable* ways).
18. Labor & academic power — deskilling, who profits, AI as austerity lever vs.
    "it replaces teachers wholesale."
19. Corporate concentration & dependence — real vs. abstention; the answer is
    public/open alternatives.
20. The environment, precisely — hyperlocal water/siting & opacity vs. both
    individual-use guilt *and* aggregate apocalypse. (the cleanest teaching case)
21. "Theft" / consent — and what the licenses actually say (Cirasella);
    licenses point toward permission; the field's move is reciprocity.

**Part IV — Your OER work, and refusal (Stefano, ~6 min)**
22. [divider] Part IV — So what about your OER work?
23. Academic integrity — real disruption to assessment vs. detector-policing/
    bans; the work is redesigning assessment. (teacher-facing; opens discussion)
24. Where AI genuinely helps OER — accessibility/alt-text, digitization/OCR,
    drafting, translation & remix. (concrete, values-aligned wins)
25. Judging appropriate use — human-accountable, disclosed (GC tool), verified;
    the output is yours when your contribution is real.
26. One honest example — a real use that worked *and* where it broke (seams
    visible; non-Toro). **[NEEDS: which project/story.]**
27. Informed refusal *and* informed adoption — both legitimate when reasoned;
    you can say exactly why.

**Launch the discussion (all)**
28. Openers for the 30-minute session — their classrooms, where a course
    assistant helps or hurts, what refusal looks like in their field, what
    they've wanted to build. Several prompts, sized to sustain 30 min.
