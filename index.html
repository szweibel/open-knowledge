I Part I

What AI Actually Is

Enough to judge it for yourself

Before we start

We’re not here to sell you AI.

We teach too. This hour is here to help you decide how to use these tools in your own work, and when to turn them down. The Graduate Center’s own AI guidance starts in the same place: human judgment first, and room to say no.

We’re neither evangelists nor alarmists. What we can offer is a clear look at how the tools work, and an honest read on which worries hold up.

Start where you are

You’ve probably used one

ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, Gemini. The basic experience is familiar: you type something, and it types back.

That part is easy. What’s worth understanding is what’s happening when it answers — because that’s what tells you when to trust it.

The one idea

It predicts the next piece of text

When the model answers, it isn’t looking anything up. It is predicting, one piece at a time, what text would plausibly come next after yours.

That single move is the whole engine. Everything else is built on it.

Tokens
Your text is split into word-sized pieces
Numbers
Pieces become numbers the model can do math on
Weighing
It weighs which earlier words matter most
Next piece → repeat
Pick a likely next piece, then do it again
Clearing the fog

What it is not

Most of the confusion comes from treating it as something it isn’t.

Not a database

It doesn’t store and retrieve facts. There’s no record in there to look them up in.

Not a mind

It doesn’t understand or intend. It models how words tend to follow words.

Not a search engine

On its own it isn’t consulting the web. It’s generating what sounds right.

The catch

“Plausible” doesn’t mean “true”

Because it’s built to produce what sounds right, it can sound completely sure and still be wrong.

It invents things

Citations, quotes, functions, sources that never existed — delivered as fluently as the true parts.

It carries its patterns

Including the skews in the text it learned from. The output reflects its sources, unevenly.

Hold onto this. Most of the real questions about AI come back to the gap between sounds right and is right.

II Part II

From Chatbot to Agent

What it can do now, and why that raises the stakes

The problem

Fluent, but not grounded

Ask a plain model for today’s weather and it makes a confident attempt — a fluent guess with nothing behind it.

It has no way to reach today. It can only produce text that looks like a weather answer.

The fix

To do better, it reaches outside itself

Newer systems can pull in information instead of guessing:

  • Search the web
  • Read a file you give it
  • Run code and read the result

Given real sources, the same question gets a real answer. This is the honest fix for the guessing problem.

The building block

A “tool” is just something it can call

The system hands the model a short list of things it can do, and the model picks one when it seems useful. That’s all “tool use” means.

🔍 Search

Query the web, a catalog, or a repository

📄 Fetch

Read a file, a page, or a record you point it at

⚙️ Run

Execute code and read back the result

The key idea

“Agentic” means it loops

Plain chatbot: you ask, it answers.

Agentic: it keeps going. It looks at a result, decides what to do next, calls another tool, and repeats until the task seems done.

That’s the whole shift — a system that takes several steps toward a goal on its own.

A few steps, chained

What that looks like

“Chart the unemployment rate for the last two years.”

  • Searches for recent figures
  • Pulls the monthly data from a source
  • Writes a small program to chart it
  • Returns a chart built from the data it pulled

← The request, and what it produced.

The steps
Search, fetch, write code, deliver.
The result
A chart built from data it actually pulled.
The honest part

You can build by describing

Describe what you want in plain language and let the agent make the change — here, “change the site’s font to Wingdings.” You can make working things without writing every line.

Which is exactly why the stakes go up. The more it does on its own, the more you have to read and check what comes back — more to verify, and more that you’re accountable for.

The instruction
Plain English, and the agent edits the file.
The result
Exactly what was asked. Checking it is on you.
III Part III

Which Worries Hold Up?

Getting precise, in both directions

How to read the rest

Let’s get precise — together

There’s a lot of noise about AI, hyped and panicked in roughly equal measure. For each worry you’ve heard, two questions help:

What holds up?

The part of the concern that’s real and worth acting on.

Where does the headline overshoot?

The part the loudest version inflates or gets wrong.

The thing we’re arguing with is the noisy public conversation, not you. Uncritical refusal and uncritical hype are both just imprecise.

Which worries hold up?

Reliability and “slop”

What holds up

It can be fluently, confidently wrong, and a flood of low-effort output really does degrade the shared pool of writing we all draw on.

Where the headline overshoots

“It’s all slop” is as lazy as “it’s magic.” The skill is knowing when a plausible draft is enough and when you need a verified source.

Which worries hold up?

Bias

What holds up

It reflects and can amplify the skews in its training data, and the harm lands unevenly across groups.

Where the headline overshoots

“It’s biased, so don’t use it” stops too early. It’s biased in ways you can often name and check — and human and institutional sources are biased too. Scrutiny beats avoidance.

Which worries hold up?

Labor and academic power

What holds up

The real questions are about power and money — deskilling pressure, who profits, the people who labeled the data, and AI used as a lever to do more with fewer of us.

Where the headline overshoots

“It replaces teachers and writers wholesale” isn’t what’s happening. The near-term reality is task-shift and pressure — a labor and policy fight, not a fact about the tool.

Which worries hold up?

Corporate concentration

What holds up

A handful of companies control the models, the terms, and the prices, and dependence on them is a real risk.

Where the headline overshoots

Abstaining doesn’t answer concentration — it just cedes the ground. The answer is public and open alternatives, which is part of why this lab exists.

Which worries hold up?

The environmental cost

What holds up

Data centers can strain local water and power, especially where they’re sited in dry regions, and the industry is often secretive about it. That’s a siting and governance problem worth pressure.

Where the headline overshoots

Two inflated versions: that one person’s use matters much (it’s a rounding error), and that the totals are apocalyptic (they’re small next to power generation and farming). Refusing a chatbot doesn’t touch the real issue.

Which worries hold up?

“They trained on our work”

What holds up

The unease is fair. A lot of open and public writing was used for training without anyone being asked, and that sits badly.

Where the headline overshoots

The open licenses we use were written to permit broad reuse, and a notice can’t un-grant that. Work you edit and shape is still yours to license. The field’s actual move is reciprocity — credit and contribution back — not a blockade.

IV Part IV

Your OER Work

Judging use, and the right to refuse

Closest to home

Academic integrity

What holds up

It genuinely disrupts how we assess writing, and writing is where a lot of thinking happens. That’s a real pedagogical problem.

Where the headline overshoots

Detectors and bans aren’t the answer — the detectors aren’t reliable enough to accuse a student, and bans don’t hold. The work is redesigning what and how we assess.

The Graduate Center’s own guidance says as much: detector scores shouldn’t be treated as proof of misconduct.

The useful part

Where it genuinely helps OER

For making and adapting open materials, a few uses are real wins — as long as a person owns the result.

♿

Accessibility

Draft alt text and captions, then check them. A tedious, real obligation it can speed up.

📄

Digitization

Turn scanned pages and old PDFs into editable, publishable text.

✎

Drafting & translation

First drafts, summaries, and rough translations you revise into shape.

The throughline

Judging appropriate use

A few questions that travel across every case:

Accountable

A person stands behind the result — and it’s theirs to license once they’ve shaped it.

Disclosed

Say where AI helped. The lab’s disclosure tool keeps this light.

Verified

Check what it produced against a real source before it ships.

Grounded

Give it your materials rather than trusting it to know.

One honest example

ThinkWith

A first-year writing instructor, Nicole Walker, built this after our workshops. A student pastes in a source passage, a cultural artifact, and a paragraph connecting them; the model gives feedback against a four-stage scale, from inert juxtaposition to real synthesis.

What worked

The AI doesn’t write the paragraph. It keeps asking the student to revise until the claim actually needs both the source and the artifact. The thinking, and the authorship, stay with the student.

Where it breaks

The judge is itself a model scoring a rubric — it can misread synthesis, so the teacher’s standard, not the tool’s, is the real backstop. And Walker names the deeper cost plainly: building it meant “committing all the sins of instrumentalist thinking.”

A person designed it and owns the standard; the model only speeds the revision loop.

Where this lands

Informed refusal and informed adoption

Both are real choices. The point of the last half hour was to make either one something you can defend.

Adopt, with reasons

Use it where it earns its place — accountable, disclosed, verified — and say why.

Refuse, with reasons

Decline it where it doesn’t fit, and say why. The GC guidance leaves room for exactly this.
